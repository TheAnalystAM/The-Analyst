European defense stocks dip as Trump trade winds down
By Aidan Williams
11/15/24 • 5 minute read
In This Article: RR., BA., SAAB_B, LDO, RHM,
7th Army Training Command - Flickr

Shares of European defense stocks fell this week, giving back gains made after President-elect Donald Trump’s electoral victory over Democratic nominee Kamala Harris.

Investors who initially welcomed Trump’s campaign pledge to pressure NATO allies to boost military spending are now grappling with the uncertainty a second Trump administration could bring. His early cabinet picks have only heightened concerns, with many viewing the controversial selections as early warning of a potentially turbulent term.

Analysts are also raising alarms that the sector may face fallout if Trump can deliver on his repeated vow to end the war in Ukraine shortly after taking office. If that happens, debt-laden European countries are expected to slash military expansion plans, intensifying competition among defense firms for a shrinking pool of procurement contracts.

Shares of U.K.-based Rolls-Royce and BAE Systems fell 6.11% and 9.4%, respectively, over the past week. Sweden’s Saab ADR declined 2.75%, while Italy’s Leonardo S.p.A dropped 5.48%. Germany-based Rheinmetall was a rare exception to an otherwise sector-wide selloff, with shares rising 0.82%.

Despite longstanding criticism that Europe has underfunded its militaries, NATO’s European members now spend about 50% more on defense than a decade ago. Still, the International Institute for Strategic Studies warned in a report this week that “much remains to be done” if Europe hopes to deter Russia, which allocates more than 7% of its GDP to defense.

“Defense-industrial capacities were reduced because of political decisions made by governments,” the London-based think tank wrote. “These same governments now need to rediscover the ‘muscle memory’ of defense and security, ensuring sustained policy attention and investment to meet the new strategic realities in Europe.”

Rare moment of weakness

Even with this week’s pullback, Europe’s defense sector continues to outperform the broader market, with shares of several firms having climbed as much as 150% amid the Russia-Ukraine War and Isreal-Gaza Conflict. Military spending rose 6.8% globally last year, its sharpest increase since 2009, lifting revenues for contractors producing drones, tanks, jets and missiles.

While defense spending hikes are broadly popular among European electorates, meeting new spending targets remains challenging for fiscally constrained nations. Germany, one of the EU’s most vocal advocates for raising defense outlays, earmarked €53 billion ($55.9 billion) for military spending in its 2025 budget, €5 billion less than Defense Minister Boris Pistorius had requested.

“This will in no way do justice to the current threat level and certainly not to Germany's responsibility in the world,” said André Wüstner, chairman of the Bundeswehrverband, an association representing more than 200,000 armed forces members.

Even so, European defense spending appears poised to rise next year as more governments shift toward bolstering their militaries. France, despite enacting broad federal budget cuts, raised its 2025 defense budget to €50.4 billion ($55 billion) from €47.2 billion this year. Poland also boosted its military spending despite domestic pressures, increasing its defense budget to a record 187 billion zylots ($48.7 billion) from 159 billion in 2024.

“We must promise today to our compatriots, but also to the soldiers of the Polish Armed Forces, that we will build a most modern military, because today, innovation is power,” Polish Prime Minister Donald Tusk said at a military parade in Warsaw following the budget announcement.
