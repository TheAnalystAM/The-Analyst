After 400% rally, Palantir stock stumbles amid valuation concerns
Blockbuster earnings growth isn’t enough to justify Palantir’s sky-high valuation, according to analysts
By Aidan Williams
09/09/25 • 3 minute read
In This Article: PLTR

Shares of Palantir are under pressure amid concerns that a sustained rally has left the company “‘wildly” overvalued.

The data analytics firm has benefited substantially from the AI boom, with shares rising over 1,700% since its 2020 public debut on the New York Stock Exchange. Having secured lucrative contracts with the U.S. government, the military and Big Tech, Palantir has been at the forefront of leveraging AI to streamline information analysis.

While the company’s growth has been staggering, with revenues rising 48% in the last quarter alone, many analysts believe Palantir’s strong performance is no longer enough to justify its roughly $367 billion valuation.

Despite a nearly 16% decline in share price from its mid-August closing high of $184.37, Palantir trades at roughly 600 times trailing earnings and 90 times sales, making it one of the most expensive large-cap stocks ever.

“We commend the strong execution, but valuation at 74 times 2026 projected annual revenue is disconnected from even optimistic growth scenarios,” Jeffries analyst Brent Thill said in a recent note. Thill maintains an underperform rating on Palantir stock and has set a $60 per share target, implying an over 60% decline from its current share price.

Mizuho analyst Gregg Moskowitz, who has a hold rating on Palantir stock and a $165 price target, echoed the sentiment, calling Palantir’s execution “stunning” but its valuation “dramatically above anything else in software.”

Some analysts are more optimistic. Wedbush’s Dan Ives sees the firm “growing into” its market cap.

“We believe Palantir has a golden path to become the next Oracle,” Ives said, referencing the software and cloud provider that has seen similar, albeit more gradual, valuation growth over the past two decades.

Ives maintains an outperform rating with a $200 price target.

Palantir co-founder and CEO Alex Karp

Yet for Palantir to match such expectations, it would have to exceed its already impressive growth

In a note titled “Could Palantir be the best short idea?”Trivariate Research CEO Adam Parker found that Palantir would need to multiply revenue by 5.6, or about 40% annually over the next five years, to reach Google’s peak price-to-sales ratio in 2005.

Over the past five years, Palantir’s revenue has increased 3.9-fold, with average annual growth of roughly 30%.

“Palantir is the single most overvalued stock I’ve seen in my career,” Parker recently said during a podcast interview, “We think people who own the stock should be selling it, and those who don’t should be shorting it.”

History of collapse

Palantir’s trailing P/E ratio of 600 well exceeds some of the highest tech valuations in recent history.

Cisco, the last large-cap tech firm to see Palantir-like valuation growth, traded at nearly 230 times trailing earnings in 2000 during the Dot Com boom. The internet infrastructure firm briefly became the world’s most valuable company, valued at over $1 trillion in today’s dollars.

Yet as the Dot Com bubble burst and the firm’s solid-but-unspectacular growth failed to impress, shares plunged 70% between 2000 and 2001.

Cisco’s market cap now sits at $264 billion, a little over a quarter of its peak, with a trailing P/E of 26.

The company’s trajectory serves as a warning for Palantir investors, particularly if the AI bubble bursts, as some in Silicon Valley predict.

“When bubbles happen, smart people get overexcited about a kernel of truth,” OpenAI CEO Sam Altman told reporters in August. “Are we in a phase where investors as a whole are overexcited about AI? My opinion is yes.”
