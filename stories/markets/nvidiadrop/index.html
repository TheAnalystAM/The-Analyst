Nvidia stock retreats as inflation fears overcome CES keynote optimism
By Aidan Williams
01/07/25 • 3 minute read
In This Article: NVDA, VGT
Flickr

Nvidia stock retreated from record highs on Tuesday as fears of persistent inflation overcame investor optimism following Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang’s CES 2025 keynote speech.

Shares of the chipmaker declined 7.5% to $141.60 from an all-time opening high of $153.09. Tuesday’s open marked the first time since mid-November that shares reached record-high levels.

Nvidia was far from the only stock to shed gains. Large-cap tech stocks led a market sell-off after stronger-than-expected job openings and service industry data stoked investor fears about stubborn inflation and limited Federal Reserve rate cuts.

New data from the Bureau of Labor Statistics showed there were 8.1 million jobs open at the end of November. The figure is higher than the 7.74 million job openings analysts polled by Bloomberg had expected and marks the highest level of openings since May 2023.

U.S. services sector activity accelerated in December as the nonmanufacturing purchasing managers index (PMI) increased to 54.1. The rise surpassed the 53.3 PMI economists surveyed by Reuters had forecast and, combined with Tuesday’s job openings data, suggests strong lingering inflation continues to grip the U.S. economy.

A bullish vision

Nvidia surged in opening hours Tuesday following Huang’s CES 2025 speech in Las Vegas. Huang took on what many considered a strongly bullish tone as he applauded the company’s advancements in gaming graphics cards, compact superchips and the development of robots and self-driving cars.

“The [autonomous vehicle] revolution has arrived,” Huang said during his keynote speech Monday, “I predict this will likely be the first multi-trillion-dollar robotics industry.”

A Nvidia sign outside the company's headquarters in Santa Clara. The chipmaker has seen its stock rise by more than 2,000% over the past five years thanks to its dominance in AI chips. (photo credit: Flickr)

Huang’s presentation comes after nearly three months of stagnant price action on a stock that rocketed more than 100% in early 2024. Despite strong quarterly earnings and frequent progress updates from the company, sky-high growth expectations seem to be already baked into the company’s valuation, hindering growth.

"Investors have become accustomed to huge beats from this company, but doing that is getting harder and harder," Ryan Detrick, chief market strategist at Carson Group, told Reuters in November.

Despite headwinds, many analysts remain bullish on Nvidia. J.P. Morgan analyst Harlan Sur reiterated a “Buy” rating on Tuesday and set a price target of $170. At the same time, Bank of America reaffirmed a “Buy” rating on Nvidia shares last Friday, disclosing a price target of $190.
