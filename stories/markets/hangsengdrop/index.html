Hong Kong stocks log worst single-day drop since 2008 as stimulus hopes sink
By Aidan Williams
10/08/24 • 2 minute read
In This Article: HSI, 3690, 1211, 700, 857, EL, WYNN
Wikimedia Commons

Hong Kong’s stocks experienced their worst decline since the 2008 Global Financial Crisis after China signaled Tuesday its unwillingness to boost the domestic economy using major stimulus initiatives, dashing investor hopes.

The Hang Seng, Hong Kong’s benchmark index, dropped 9.4% on Tuesday, the worst single-day drop since Oct. 27, 2008, when it sank 11%. The benchmark had previously been up 34% for the month.

Leading the decline was Chinese food delivery giant Meituan (3690), which sunk 15.5%, and Chinese electric automaker BYD (1211), which shed 8.9%. State-owned oil and gas company PetroChina (857) dropped 7.6%, while multinational technology conglomerate Tencent (700) declined 8.3%.

The decline comes after China’s National Development and Reform Commission outlined economic recovery plans that lacked major spending initiatives. The planning agency announced 200 billion yuan ($28.3 billion) in domestic spending, far below analysts' estimates of multi-trillion yuan investments.

The disappointing stimulus plans cast doubt on China’s ability to meet its 5% annual growth target. NDRC Chairman Zhang Shangjie said China was still “fully confident” it could meet its target, however, he acknowledged the economy faces difficulties and an increasingly “more complex and extreme” global environment.

The disappointment in China had global effects, sinking shares of European and American companies with deep business ties to China. Most notably, Estee Lauder (EL) fell 2.2% while Wynn Resorts (WYNN) lost 3.3%.
