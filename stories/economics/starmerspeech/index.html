UK PM Starmer warns of 'painful' October budget ahead as public finances face shortfalls
By Aidan Williams
08/28/24 • 3 minute read
In This Article: FTSE 100, FTSE All-Share
The Guardian

UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer warned on Tuesday the government’s October budget will be “painful” as the administration works to control a $29 billion (£22 billion) financing shortfall.

“It's not the position I want to be in,” Starmer said in the rose garden of 10 Downing Street, the prime minister’s residence, “But we have to end the politics of the easy answer that solves nothing.”

While government budget cuts are expected, Starmer, who won his PM bid in a landslide victory in early July, is likely to raise taxes to help counter the “black hole” deficit. The PM reiterated in his Tuesday speech that the government would not raise taxes on national social safety net contributions, income tax, or value-added tax.

“Those with the broadest shoulders should bear the heaviest burden,” Starmer said to a gathered crowd of voters, “those who made the mess should have to do their bit to clean up.”

The FTSE 100, an index of the Londons Stock Exchange’s most highly capitalized blue chip stocks, rose .85% in the hours following Starmer’s announcement. The FTSE All-Share increased by .78%.

Starmer’s Labour government has been aided by record low inflation rates and increasing GDP growth in recent months, which led his administration to unveil costly plans to increase National Health Service funding, nationalize the country’s rail system and establish Great British Energy, Labour’s proposed public energy corporation. With a “worse” than “ever imagined” budget deficit now on the books, the administration's plans are under threat of being scaled back.

In its electoral manifesto, Labour said it would raise £7.35 billion by 2028-29 to fund public services by closing tax loopholes on private equity and non-domiciled individuals — or those whose domiciles lie outside the country for tax purposes — ending tax breaks for independent schools and introducing a “time-limited windfall tax” on oil and gas firms. However, analysts are unsure whether these measures could be implemented depending on how parents and big energy firms respond.
