Amid political turmoil, Bank of Korea to expand short-term liquidity to stabilize markets
By Aidan Williams
12/04/24 • 3 minute read
In This Article: KRWUSD
Flickr

The Bank of Korea announced Wednesday that it will expand short-term liquidity to stabilize foreign exchange markets following the brief installation of martial law Tuesday night.

The Bank’s Monetary Policy Board said it will begin to “conduct irregular” acquisitions of repurchase agreements from banks until liquidity is sufficiently available and “markets are fully stabilized.”

The announcement came after South Korean Finance Minister Choi Sang-mok convened an emergency meeting of the Monetary Policy Board Wednesday morning. The Board pledged to provide “unlimited liquidity support” until markets return to normal.

The benchmark Kospi index fell nearly 2% in afternoon trading Tuesday while big bank stocks shed over 5% of their value, a reflection of general market unease. The South Korean Won, which was already the worst performing Asian currency against the dollar, sank 2.8% before slightly rebounding.

While most of the market sold off, South Korean social media and payment app giant Kakao jumped more than 7% amidst a potential shift in antitrust policy. Yoon had previously called the app’s dominant rideshare service “a tyranny.”

While the Bank of Korea said market conditions are expected to improve thanks to the “solid fundamentals of the Korean economy,” the Bank said it will closely monitor market conditions to determine if heightened intervention is needed.

South Korea president Yoon Suk Yeol shocked the Southeast Asian nation late Tuesday night by declaring martial law during a surprise press conference. Yoon, who is deeply unpopular and rules over a bitterly divided government, stated the declaration was necessary to counter the an opposition bent on “trying to overthrow the free democracy.”

Politicians on both sides of the aisle, including members of Yoon’s own right-wing People Power Party quickly condemned the declaration, with People Power Party leader Han Dong-hoon calling the declaration of martial law “wrong.” The National Assembly convened to reject the declaration within hours of Yoon’s announcement.

South Korea’s main opposition Democratic Party and five smaller parties jointly submitted a motion to impeach Yoon on Wednesday. The impeachment would require the support of two-thirds of parliament and at least six of nine Constitutional Court justices to pass. The bill could be voted on as early as Friday.
