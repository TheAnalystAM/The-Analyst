Elliot, Southwest enter early settlement discussions after months of sparring, sources say
By Aidan Williams
10/20/24 • 4 minute read
In This Article: LUV
Wikimedia Commons

Elliot Investment Management and Southwest Airlines (LUV) have entered settlement discussions in a bid to stave off a proxy battle over control of the airline’s board, Bloomberg News reported on Saturday, citing people familiar with the matter.

The hedge fund is seeking a deal that would give them representation on Southwest’s board, but not control. The board will decrease to 12 members after Chairman Gary Kelly steps down next year, meaning Elliot will likely receive fewer than six selections.

The talks, which progressed through Saturday, are preliminary and could fall through, sources say. While Southwest has previously offered Elliot some concessions, until now the airline has refused to discuss leadership changes.

On Monday, Elliot Management called for a special shareholder meeting on Dec. 10. The fund said it hoped to replace eight chairmen and take command of the airline’s board.

Southwest responded by saying it had attempted to contact Elliot Management in the hopes of avoiding a fight. The airline stated the shareholder meeting was scheduled to cause “maximum disruption” before a busy travel season.

Elliot Management Corporate founder and CEO Paul Singer speaks at the 2014 annual meeting of the World Economic Forum. Under his leadership, Elliot has pushed for sweeping changes at coffee chain Starbucks, Salesforce and Twitter. (photo credit: Flickr)

Elliot Management, which is one of Wall Street's biggest and busiest activist investors, surprised shareholders when it took an 11% stake in the Dallas-based airline in June. Since then, the hedge fund has fought a highly publicized battle to remove board members, fire CEO Bob Jordan and review the company’s financial strategies in an effort to boost the company’s financial performance.

Shares of Southwest are down nearly 47% since the COVID pandemic began back in March of 2020.

Southwest stock surged after an investor event last month in which the company laid out a 3-year plan that Jordan claimed would add $4 billion in earnings before interest and taxes by 2027.

The proposal, which marked the biggest change to Southwest's operating structure in 50 years, included partnerships, vacation packages for customers and aircraft sale-leasebacks. In response to shifting consumer preferences, the airline decided to end Southwest’s longstanding open-seating policy and planned to add extra legroom seats to flights.

Elliot remained unimpressed by the changes, stating the plan was “filled with long-dated promises of better performance.”

Southwest planes sit idle on the tarmac amidst the airline's Winter 2022 booking system meltdown (photo credit: FML News)

The hedge fund has been particularly insistent on its calls to oust Jordan from his post, who they accused of “playing with shareholders’ money.”

“We believe that competent new leaders, working through a deliberate and thoughtful process, should chart the course forward for Southwest,” Elliott partner John Pike and portfolio manager Bobby Xu said on Monday in a letter to shareholders.

Southwest has so far stood behind Jordan, who has been at the airline for more than four decades. His goal was to increase airline revenues and better compete against competitors.

Southwest reports third-quarter results on Thursday. The company raised its Q3 revenue forecast late last month.
